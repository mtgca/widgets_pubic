Inference Strategies: Three Ways to Read the Same Model

A trained language model never tells you what the next word is. It hands you a probability distribution over every word it knows — and then it's your job to pick one. That picking rule is the decoding (inference) strategy, and it changes everything: the same model can sound robotic, careful, or creative depending only on how you choose. Below, one tiny toy model is decoded three different ways. Watch how the outputs diverge.

The setup

A toy language model you can see all of

Real models have ~50,000 words in their vocabulary and effectively infinite sentences. Our toy model starts every sentence with The, knows a handful of words, and finishes after two more picks. Because it is tiny, we can draw its entire tree of possible sentences — something impossible for a real LLM — and check which strategy actually finds the best one.

The probability of a whole sentence is the product of the step-by-step probabilities (the chain rule):

P(y1,…,yT∣x) = ∏t=1T P(yt∣y<t,x)

Keep that in mind: a sentence that starts with a slightly less likely word can still end up more likely overall — if its later words are strong. That single fact is why the three strategies below disagree.

Strategy 1 · Greedy decoding

"Just take the biggest number, every time"

One path. No looking back, no looking ahead. Fast, deterministic — and short-sighted.

Analogy Greedy decoding is a chess player who always captures the most valuable piece available right now, never thinking one move ahead. Each capture looks great in the moment — and walks straight into checkmate.

At every step, greedy picks the single most probable token and commits to it forever:

yt= argmaxy P(y∣y<t,x)

You may have played this on your phone: start a sentence, then keep tapping the top autocomplete suggestion. That's greedy decoding with a real language model. Try it:

✅ Pros
  • Fastest possible — one forward pass per token, no extra candidates to track.
  • Deterministic — same prompt, same output, every time. Great for debugging and reproducible pipelines.
  • Surprisingly fine for short, factual completions where one answer dominates.
❌ Cons
  • Local-optimum traps — a great first word can lead into a weak sentence (Lab 1).
  • Repetition loops — once a loop enters the context, the same context keeps producing the same pick, forever (Lab 2).
  • Dull, generic text in open-ended generation — it never takes an interesting risk.
Strategy 2 · Beam search

"Keep the k best storylines alive"

Multiple explored paths. Still deterministic — but far less short-sighted.

Analogy Send k friends into a maze down the k most promising corridors. At every junction, everyone reports back, you rank all the options seen so far, and again keep only the k best storylines — even if that means abandoning a corridor that looked great at the entrance.

Beam search keeps k partial hypotheses (the beam width). Each step it extends all of them, scores every candidate by the probability of the whole sequence so far — in practice the sum of log-probabilities — and keeps the top k:

score(y1,…,yt) = ∑i=1t log⁡P(yi∣y<i,x)

🔦 Lab 3 — Same model, wider flashlight
Beam width k:

Tree view — kept beams vs. pruned branches

Amber = hypotheses alive in the beam. Faded = pruned. Set k=1 and beam search collapses into greedy — that's not a coincidence, greedy is beam search with a beam of one.

✅ Pros
  • Escapes greedy's traps — a slightly-worse first word can survive long enough to prove itself.
  • Near-standard for machine translation and speech recognition, where a "correct" output exists.
  • Still deterministic and tunable: bigger k → wider search.
❌ Cons
  • k× the compute and memory — every step extends and re-scores k hypotheses.
  • Still no randomness: in open-ended generation it converges on generic, repetitive "safest" text.
  • It's an approximation, not a guarantee — the true best sequence can still be pruned early.
Strategy 3 · Nucleus sampling (top-p)

"Roll dice — but only inside the trustworthy zone"

Controlled randomness. The candidate pool grows and shrinks with the model's confidence.

Analogy You're ordering at a restaurant. Instead of always ordering the #1 bestseller (greedy — boring) or picking any random line on the menu (pure sampling — you might get the deep-fried napkin), you cover the bottom of the menu with your hand and pick at random among the dishes that make up, say, 90% of what people actually order.

Sort the vocabulary by probability, take the smallest set whose probabilities add up to at least p — the nucleus — renormalize inside it, and sample:

Vp= smallest set with ∑y∈Vp P(y∣y<t,x) ≥p

✅ Pros
  • Adaptive — confident model → tiny pool (nearly greedy); uncertain model → wide pool (real variety). A fixed top-k can't do this.
  • Cuts the crazy tail — the 0.5%-probability nonsense tokens are excluded before the dice roll.
  • The default for chatbots and creative generation — diverse but coherent.
❌ Cons
  • Not reproducible — same prompt, different output each run (unless you fix the random seed).
  • No optimality story: it isn't even trying to find the most probable sequence.
  • Badly tuned p (too high) lets weird tokens back in; too low and it degenerates into greedy.
