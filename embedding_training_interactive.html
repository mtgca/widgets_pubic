How Word Embeddings Are Learned

Nobody writes embedding vectors by hand. They start as random numbers and are shaped by one simple training signal: predict the next word. Here you will train a tiny model on a tiny dataset — step by step — and watch meaningful geometry emerge from noise.

1A tiny dataset — and where the training pairs come from

Our whole “corpus” is 14 short sentences. There are no human labels: every consecutive pair of words becomes one training example (input word → the word that actually came next). The text supervises itself. Click a sentence to see the pairs it contributes.

    2The model: embedding lookup → linear layer → next-word probabilities

    Each vocabulary word owns one row of an embedding table E∈R11×8 — d=8 learned numbers per word. A linear layer turns that row into one score per vocabulary word, and softmax turns scores into probabilities. All numbers below are live — they show the model as it is right now, and they change when you train it in step 3. Click an input word to follow it through.

    3Train it — and watch the embedding space organize itself

    One epoch = one pass over all 34 pairs: the model predicts, the loss measures its surprise, and gradients nudge W, b and the embedding rows to make the true next words more likely. The 8-D embedding vectors can’t be drawn directly, so the map below projects them onto their two directions of largest variance (PCA), with trails.     Where does the “learning” of an embedding happen?
    The map above is a PCA projection: the 8-D vectors are flattened onto the 2-D plane that preserves the most variance. This is exactly what you do for real models too (with PCA, t-SNE, UMAP…), since their embeddings have hundreds of dimensions.

    Principle Words used in similar contexts get similar vectors. The model never sees definitions — only which words follow which. Distributional similarity becomes geometric closeness.

    Gotcha
    Tied weights Half the parameters, one constraint. Sharing drops this model from 187 to 99 parameters — in a real LM tying saves an entire |V|×d matrix (GPT-2 ties exactly like this). The price: E[y]·E[x] is symmetric, so “like after i” and “i after like” can’t be scored independently — only the bias breaks the tie — and on this asymmetric little corpus the loss floors near 1.06 instead of 0.91.
