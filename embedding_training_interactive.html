How Word Embeddings Are Learned

Nobody writes embedding vectors by hand. They start as random numbers and are shaped by one simple training signal: predict the next word. Here you will train a tiny model on a tiny dataset — step by step — and watch meaningful geometry emerge from noise.

1A tiny dataset — and where the training pairs come from

Our whole “corpus” is 14 short sentences. There are no human labels: every consecutive pair of words becomes one training example (input word → the word that actually came next). The text supervises itself. Click a sentence to see the pairs it contributes.

    2The model: embedding lookup → linear layer → next-word probabilities

    Each vocabulary word owns one row of an embedding table E∈R11×2 (we use d=2 dimensions so we can draw the space). A linear layer turns that row into one score per vocabulary word, and softmax turns scores into probabilities.     3Train it — and watch the embedding space organize itself

    One epoch = one pass over all 34 pairs: the model predicts, the loss measures its surprise, and gradients nudge W, b and the embedding rows to make the true next words more likely.     Where does the “learning” of an embedding happen? Each pair (x → y) sends a gradient back through the linear layer into exactly one row of the table: ∂L∂E[x] — the row of the current input word. All other rows are untouched by that pair. Two words that are followed by the same kinds of words keep receiving the same kinds of nudges, so their rows converge to nearby points. Meaning is never given to the model — it precipitates out of prediction errors.
    Here the space is genuinely 2-D, so this map is the embedding space. Real models use hundreds of dimensions; to draw a map like this you would first project them down (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP…).

    Principle Words used in similar contexts get similar vectors. The model never sees definitions — only which words follow which. Distributional similarity becomes geometric closeness.
    Look for it After training, the pronouns i / you / we land near each other (all are followed by like, love, eat, hate…). Even more striking: love and hate end up almost on top of each other — opposite meanings, but identical contexts (both followed by pizza or pasta). Embeddings capture how words are used, not what they mean to us. And like drifts away from love/hate because it alone is also followed by “to”.
    Gotcha “pasta” never moves. In these 14 sentences, pasta only ever appears as the last word, so it is never an input — its embedding row never receives a gradient and stays at its random start. A word’s vector is only as good as the contexts it is used in. (Check its trail on the map!)
